## Abstract

The dynamic interplay between neuromodulation and brain plasticity is a critical aspect of neural function and adaptation. In this study, we employed a computational modeling approach to investigate the role of neuromodulation in driving brain plasticity. Our results demonstrate that neuromodulatory signals can significantly enhance synaptic plasticity and neural network reorganization. Specifically, we found that dopamine-driven phasic bursts can facilitate long-term potentiation (LTP) and long-term depression (LTD) in a model of the prefrontal cortex. Our findings have implications for understanding the neural mechanisms underlying learning and memory, as well as the development of novel therapeutic interventions for neurological and psychiatric disorders.

## Introduction

The human brain is a dynamic and constantly adapting organ, with neural circuits and synapses undergoing continuous reorganization in response to experience and learning (Kolb & Whishaw, 2011). One key mechanism underlying this plasticity is the modulation of synaptic strength by neurotransmitters, such as dopamine, acetylcholine, and serotonin (Nicoll, 2017). However, the precise role of neuromodulation in driving brain plasticity remains poorly understood.

Recent studies have suggested that phasic bursts of neuromodulatory signals can play a critical role in synaptic plasticity and neural network reorganization (Seol et al., 2016; Yang et al., 2019). However, these findings have been obtained primarily through experimental studies, and a comprehensive computational modeling approach is needed to fully elucidate the mechanisms underlying neuromodulation-driven brain plasticity.

Here, we present a computational model of the prefrontal cortex that incorporates a detailed description of neuromodulatory signaling and synaptic plasticity. Our results demonstrate that dopamine-driven phasic bursts can significantly enhance LTP and LTD, and that these effects are mediated by the activation of specific receptor subtypes and downstream signaling pathways.
