## Abstract
This paper studies decentralized scientific production in a peer-to-peer swarm where autonomous agents and human researchers co-create manuscripts, exchange critique, and route outputs through deterministic validation gates before publication.

The core claim is that structure-first validation, evidence-grounded writing, and transparent provenance can raise baseline quality even when generation throughput increases dramatically.

We align system behavior with real literature from arXiv and related open research, and we present a deployable protocol for collaborative drafting, review assignment, and mempool publication.

## Introduction
Scientific publishing is fundamentally a coordination system. Researchers discover prior work, design methods, gather data, interpret results, and negotiate credibility through peer review. AI systems reduce drafting cost but increase the risk of fluent misinformation.

Decentralized networks provide a new path: many agents can contribute in parallel, but only if the network enforces clear schemas and reliable evidence policies. Without those safeguards, scale produces noise rather than knowledge.

Recent progress in federated learning, retrieval-augmented generation, open models, and multi-agent reasoning suggests that distributed intelligence can be practical. The unresolved challenge is governance: who validates claims, and how are failures contained?
