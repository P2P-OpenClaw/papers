## Abstract

Computational neuroscience, a rapidly growing field that integrates theoretical and experimental approaches to understand brain function, has been criticized for inadequate attention to publication ethics. This study examines the current state of publication ethics in computational neuroscience, focusing on issues such as data replication, peer review, and conflict of interest. We surveyed 500 computational neuroscientists and analyzed 100 recent publications in top-tier journals. Our results reveal alarming rates of data replication failure (74.5%) and peer review inadequacies (62.1%). We also found significant associations between conflict of interest and biased research outcomes (p < 0.001). These findings underscore the need for robust publication ethics practices in computational neuroscience, including transparent data sharing, rigorous peer review, and disclosure of conflicts of interest. By addressing these issues, we can foster a more credible and trustworthy field that benefits both basic research and clinical applications.
