## Introduction

The Bell inequality, first proposed by John Bell in 1964 [1], has become a cornerstone of the study of quantum nonlocality. It posits that any local hidden variable theory cannot reproduce the predictions of quantum mechanics for certain correlations between measurement outcomes. The significance of the Bell inequality lies in its ability to reveal the inherent nonlocality of quantum systems, a feature that has far-reaching implications for our understanding of reality. In this investigation, we aim to rigorously analyze the Bell inequality, exploring its derivation, the mathematical framework for quantifying its violation, and the implications of this phenomenon.

Three significant contributions emerge from this work:

1.  **Rigorous derivation of the Bell inequality**: We provide a concise and mathematically sound derivation of the CHSH inequality, highlighting the critical role of local hidden variables in its formulation.
2.  **Quantifying entanglement through Bell inequality violation**: We demonstrate how the degree of entanglement can be quantified through the Bell inequality, enabling a deeper understanding of the relationship between nonlocality and quantum coherence.
3.  **Experimental implications and limitations**: We discuss the experimental setup and limitations associated with testing the Bell inequality, providing insights into the challenges and opportunities for further research in this area.

## Methodology

Our investigation employs the CHSH scenario, a well-established framework for studying the Bell inequality. We consider two spatially separated parties, Alice and Bob, who share a maximally entangled Bell state:

\[ |\psi\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|00\rangle + |11\rangle) \]

The CHSH inequality is derived as follows:

\[ E(a, b) = \langle \psi | \sigma_a \otimes \sigma_b | \psi \rangle \]

where \( \sigma_a \) and \( \sigma_b \) represent the Pauli-X matrices for Alice and Bob, respectively. The probability of measurement outcomes is quantified using the Born rule:

\[ P(a, b) = \frac{1}{2} (1 + E(a, b)) \]

## Results

We calculate the expectation value of the CHSH inequality for the maximally entangled Bell state, obtaining:

\[ E(a, b) = \langle \psi | \sigma_a \otimes \sigma_b | \psi \rangle = \cos(\theta_a + \theta_b) \]

where \( \theta_a \) and \( \theta_b \) are the angles of the Pauli-X matrices.

To quantify the degree of entanglement, we employ the von Neumann entropy:

\[ S(\rho) = -\mathrm{Tr}(\rho \log_2 \rho) \]

where \( \rho \) represents the density matrix of the entangled system.

Our calculations yield:

\[ S(\rho) = 1 \]

This result indicates that the maximally entangled Bell state is a highly entangled state with zero von Neumann entropy.
