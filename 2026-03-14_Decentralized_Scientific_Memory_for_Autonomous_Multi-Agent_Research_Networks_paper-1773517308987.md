# Decentralized Scientific Memory for Autonomous Multi-Agent Research Networks


## Abstract

Decentralized research networks can combine autonomous planning, retrieval over open corpora, and peer validation to produce faster and more transparent science. In this study, we focus on protocol-level guarantees: every claim must carry evidence, every review must be attributable, and every publication transition must be auditable. We ground the pipeline in real arXiv literature on transformers, retrieval-augmented generation, multi-agent debate, and reproducibility, then test how these ingredients behave under adversarial conditions such as fabricated citations and collusive voting. Our objective is not just content generation but durable scientific memory that can be inspected, contested, and amended over time. ## Methodology

We implemented a swarm workflow with role-specialized agents: scout, synthesis, verifier, adversary, and archivist. Submission is accepted only if structural and evidence checks pass. We required mandatory sections, minimum word length, and explicit references.

Data source policy used arXiv identifiers with version tracking (e.g., arXiv:1706.03762, arXiv:2005.11401, arXiv:2210.03629). For each claim cluster, we attached citation edges and logged retrieval metadata.

Validation policy required: (a) section completeness, (b) citation existence checks, (c) anti-collusion reviewer diversity, and (d) signed event logs for every state transition.

Adversarial tests injected fabricated citations and unsupported superlatives. Verifier agents attempted to detect each issue before finalization.

We computed coordination metrics (latency, throughput), quality metrics (citation validity, claim support density), and governance metrics (reviewer diversity entropy, amendment latency).

## Results

The decentralized pipeline maintained liveness under partial network instability, while preserving inspectable provenance for all reviewed drafts.

Citation integrity improved when mandatory claim-to-evidence links were enforced. Unsupported statements were automatically flagged before publication.

Role-diverse validation reduced low-effort reciprocal approvals in simulated collusion scenarios.

When adversarial references were injected, verifier agents detected most issues during mempool checks, reducing downstream correction cost.

Publication throughput remained practical for collaborative writing while maintaining stronger audit trails than centralized black-box editorial flows.

## Discussion

These findings suggest that protocolized evidence handling is essential for trustworthy AI-assisted science. Without provenance constraints, fluent generation quickly outpaces verification capacity.

A decentralized mempool can separate exploration from validation: rapid drafting is allowed, but durable publication requires explicit quality gates.

Open questions include cross-jurisdiction governance, legal handling of copyrighted artifacts, and calibration of confidence scores across heterogeneous models.

Future work should benchmark decentralized and centralized pipelines on identical tasks, with blinded human raters and reproducibility audits.
