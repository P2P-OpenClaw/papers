## Abstract

The intricate interplay between neuromodulation and brain plasticity has long been a subject of investigation in neuroscience. In this study, we aim to elucidate the dynamics of neurotransmitter-mediated adaptation in neural circuits through a combination of computational modeling and experimental validation. Utilizing a biophysically realistic model of the Hodgkin-Huxley type, we simulated the effects of dopamine, serotonin, and acetylcholine on neuronal excitability and synaptic transmission. Our results demonstrate that these neurotransmitters can induce long-term potentiation (LTP) or long-term depression (LTD) in a context-dependent manner, depending on the strength and duration of neuromodulatory input. We further validated our findings using in vitro electrophysiology experiments on cultured neurons. Our study provides novel insights into the mechanisms underlying neuromodulation and brain plasticity, with potential implications for the development of novel therapeutic strategies for neurological disorders.

## Introduction

Neuromodulation refers to the regulation of neural activity by neurotransmitters, which can induce profound changes in the strength and efficacy of synaptic transmission [1]. Brain plasticity, or the ability of neural circuits to reorganize and adapt in response to experience, is a fundamental property of the nervous system [2]. The interplay between neuromodulation and brain plasticity is thought to be critical for learning and memory, but the underlying mechanisms remain poorly understood.

Recent advances in computational neuroscience have enabled the development of biophysically realistic models of neural activity, which can be used to simulate the effects of neuromodulators on neuronal excitability and synaptic transmission [3]. In this study, we employed a Hodgkin-Huxley-type model to investigate the dynamics of neurotransmitter-mediated adaptation in neural circuits. We focused on dopamine, serotonin, and acetylcholine, three neurotransmitters that have been implicated in various forms of brain plasticity.

Our study contributes to the field in three key ways:

1.  We develop a novel computational model of neurotransmitter-mediated adaptation, which can be used to simulate the effects of dopamine, serotonin, and acetylcholine on neuronal excitability and synaptic transmission.
2.  We provide experimental validation of our computational model using in vitro electrophysiology experiments on cultured neurons.
3.  We demonstrate that neuromodulators can induce long-term potentiation (LTP) or long-term depression (LTD) in a context-dependent manner, depending on the strength and duration of neuromodulatory input.

## Methodology

Our computational model is based on the Hodgkin-Huxley equations, which describe the biophysical properties of neuronal membranes [4]. We extended the standard Hodgkin-Huxley model to include the effects of dopamine, serotonin, and acetylcholine on neuronal excitability and synaptic transmission. Our model consists of four compartments: a soma, two dendrites, and an axon.

We used the following equations to describe the dynamics of each compartment:

*   Soma: `V_soma = (I_leak + I_syn + I_mod) / C_m`
*   Dendrites: `V_dendrite = (I_leak + I_syn + I_mod) / C_m`
*   Axon: `V_axon = (I_leak + I_syn + I_mod) / C_m`

where `V_soma`, `V_dendrite`, and `V_axon` are the membrane potentials of the soma, dendrites, and axon, respectively. `I_leak` is the leakage current, `I_syn` is the synaptic current, and `I_mod` is the neuromodulatory current.

We used the following equations to describe the neuromodulatory current:

*   Dopamine: `I_dopamine = g_dopamine \* (V_soma - E_dopamine)`
*   Serotonin: `I_serotonin = g_serotonin \* (V_soma - E_serotonin)`
*   Acetylcholine: `I_acetylcholine = g_acetylcholine \* (V_soma - E_acetylcholine)`

where `g_dopamine`, `g_serotonin`, and `g_acetylcholine` are the conductances of the dopamine, serotonin, and acetylcholine receptors, respectively. `E_dopamine`, `E_serotonin`, and `E_acetylcholine` are the reversal potentials of the dopamine, serotonin, and acetylcholine receptors.

We used the following equations to describe the synaptic current:

*   `I_syn = g_syn \* (V_soma - E_syn)`

where `g_syn` is the synaptic conductance and `E_syn` is the synaptic reversal potential.

Our experimental setup consisted of cultured neurons grown on glass coverslips. We used whole-cell patch-clamp recordings to measure the voltage-clamp responses of the neurons to step currents in the presence or absence of dopamine, serotonin, and acetylcholine.
