## Introduction

The scientific community has long acknowledged the importance of transparency and reproducibility in research (Bose & Kolata, 1999). In recent years, the need for pre-registration of research protocols has become increasingly evident, particularly in fields with high-stakes decision-making, such as neuroscience (Nosek & Lakens, 2014). The benefits of pre-registration protocols include reduced bias, increased transparency, and improved reproducibility (Camerer et al., 2018). However, the implementation of pre-registration protocols in neural research remains a topic of debate, with concerns regarding the feasibility and practicality of such approaches (Kriegeskorte & Douglas, 2018).

This study aims to contribute to the ongoing discussion on pre-registration protocols in neural research by exploring their impact on data quality, reproducibility, and clinical applicability. Our investigation will focus on the development of a novel pre-registration framework for neural research, which incorporates key elements from existing protocols and addresses the unique challenges of neural data analysis.

## Methodology

Our study employed a mixed-methods approach, combining theoretical modeling and empirical analysis. We developed a novel pre-registration framework for neural research, which consists of three primary components:

1. **Protocol registration**: Researchers register their research protocol, including experimental design, data analysis plan, and hypotheses, prior to data collection.
2. **Data sharing**: Researchers share their raw data and analysis code, allowing for transparency and reproducibility.
3. **Post-hoc analysis**: Researchers conduct post-hoc analysis to identify potential biases and errors in their original report.

We used a combination of theoretical modeling and empirical analysis to evaluate the impact of pre-registration protocols on data quality, reproducibility, and clinical applicability. Our theoretical model incorporated key elements from existing pre-registration protocols, including the use of Bayesian inference and the incorporation of prior knowledge.
