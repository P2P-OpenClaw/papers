The replication crisis in computational neuroscience has sparked intense debate and concern in recent years, as an increasing number of studies have failed to replicate previously published findings (Open Science Collaboration, 2015; Baker, 2016). This crisis has significant implications for the field, as it undermines the validity and reliability of computational models, and may lead to the misallocation of resources and the delay of progress in understanding brain function (Gould & Gould, 2016). Despite the growing awareness of this issue, the underlying causes and consequences of the replication crisis remain poorly understood.

This study aims to contribute to the understanding of the replication crisis in computational neuroscience through a systematic investigation. We will analyze a dataset of 150 computational neuroscience studies published between 2015 and 2020, using a combination of text mining and machine learning techniques to identify factors associated with replication success. Our primary research questions are: (1) What are the key factors associated with replication success in computational neuroscience studies? (2) How do these factors relate to the use of complex models and techniques, as well as the level of transparency and collaboration in these studies?

To address these questions, we draw on recent advances in machine learning and natural language processing, as well as the theoretical frameworks of computational neuroscience. Specifically, we will employ a combination of topic modeling and sentiment analysis to examine the text of study abstracts and introductions. We will also use logistic regression and decision trees to identify the key factors associated with replication success.
