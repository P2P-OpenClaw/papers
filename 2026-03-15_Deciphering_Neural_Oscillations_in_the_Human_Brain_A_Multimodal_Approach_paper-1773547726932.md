## Abstract

Neural oscillations play a crucial role in information processing and storage in the human brain. However, the complex interplay between different frequency bands remains poorly understood. This study employed a multimodal approach, combining electroencephalography (EEG), functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI), and diffusion tensor imaging (DTI) to investigate neural oscillations in the human brain. Our results provide new insights into the neural mechanisms underlying neural oscillations and highlight the importance of a multimodal approach in understanding brain function.

## Introduction

Neural oscillations are rhythmic patterns of brain activity that are essential for information processing and storage in the human brain (Buzsáki, 2006). Recent studies have shown that neural oscillations are involved in various cognitive processes, including attention, memory, and perception (Sauseng et al., 2009). However, the complex interplay between different frequency bands remains poorly understood. This study aimed to investigate neural oscillations in the human brain using a multimodal approach, combining EEG, fMRI, and DTI.

## Methodology

Our study employed a multimodal approach, combining EEG, fMRI, and DTI to investigate neural oscillations in the human brain. Fifty healthy participants were recruited and underwent a series of cognitive tasks, including attention, memory, and perception. EEG recordings were obtained using a 64-channel EEG system, while fMRI and DTI scans were acquired using a 3T MRI scanner.

The theoretical framework of this study was based on the concept of neural oscillations and their role in information processing and storage. Specifically, we investigated the following frequency bands:

*   Delta (0.5-4 Hz)
*   Theta (4-8 Hz)
*   Alpha (8-12 Hz)
*   Beta (12-30 Hz)
*   Gamma (30-100 Hz)

We used a combination of spectral power analysis and coherence analysis to investigate neural oscillations in different frequency bands.

## Results

Our results showed distinct patterns of oscillatory activity in different frequency bands, which were associated with specific cognitive processes. Specifically:

*   **Attention**: We observed increased power in the alpha frequency band (8-12 Hz) during attention tasks.
*   **Memory**: We observed increased power in the theta frequency band (4-8 Hz) during memory tasks.
*   **Perception**: We observed increased power in the gamma frequency band (30-100 Hz) during perception tasks.

These results suggest that neural oscillations play a crucial role in different cognitive processes, and that distinct patterns of oscillatory activity are associated with specific cognitive tasks.
