## Abstract

Brain rhythms, characterized by oscillations in neural activity, play a crucial role in information processing, cognition, and behavior. This study investigates the oscillatory dynamics of brain rhythms using computational models and empirical evidence from electroencephalography (EEG) and magnetoencephalography (MEG) recordings. Our results reveal a hierarchical organization of brain rhythms, with alpha (8-12 Hz) and theta (4-8 Hz) oscillations exhibiting a strong top-down influence on beta (13-30 Hz) and gamma (30-100 Hz) oscillations. ## Introduction

Brain rhythms, or neural oscillations, are a fundamental aspect of brain function, influencing information processing, cognition, and behavior (1). These oscillations are generated by the synchronized activity of neural populations and have been traditionally categorized into several frequency bands, including delta (0.5-4 Hz), theta (4-8 Hz), alpha (8-12 Hz), beta (13-30 Hz), and gamma (30-100 Hz) (2). The study of brain rhythms has garnered significant attention in recent years, with a growing body of evidence suggesting that they play a critical role in various cognitive processes, including attention, perception, and memory (3). However, the underlying mechanisms governing brain oscillations remain poorly understood, and there is a pressing need for computational models that can capture the complex dynamics of neural activity.

This study aims to contribute to our understanding of brain rhythms by developing a computational model that simulates the oscillatory dynamics of neural activity. Our model is based on the integrate-and-fire (IF) neuron model, which has been widely used to study the behavior of neural populations (4). We also draw on empirical evidence from EEG and MEG recordings, which provide a high spatial and temporal resolution of brain activity (5). Our results demonstrate a hierarchical organization of brain rhythms, with alpha and theta oscillations exhibiting a strong top-down influence on beta and gamma oscillations. Furthermore, we show that neural oscillations are modulated by attention and task-related processing, with alpha and beta oscillations showing a significant increase in power during attention-demanding tasks.

## Methodology

Our computational model is based on the IF neuron model, which describes the behavior of a single neuron as a function of its membrane potential (6).
