## Abstract

Neural oscillations and brain rhythms are fundamental to brain function, playing a crucial role in information processing, memory, and learning. However, the intricate relationship between these oscillations remains poorly understood. In this study, we employed a computational neuroscience approach to investigate the interplay between neural oscillations and brain rhythms in a simulated neural network. Using a combination of theoretical modeling, numerical simulations, and statistical analysis, we identified key features of neural oscillations that influence brain rhythms. Our results demonstrate that the interaction between different frequency bands of neural oscillations generates complex brain rhythms, which in turn modulate neural activity. ## Introduction

Neural oscillations, or rhythmic patterns of neural activity, are a hallmark of brain function. Different frequency bands of oscillations, such as alpha (8-12 Hz), beta (13-30 Hz), and gamma (30-100 Hz), have been linked to various cognitive processes, including attention, memory, and learning (Buzsáki & Wang, 2012). Brain rhythms, on the other hand, refer to the synchronized patterns of neural activity that occur across different brain regions. These rhythms are thought to play a critical role in coordinating neural activity and facilitating communication between brain regions (Fries, 2009). Despite their importance, the relationship between neural oscillations and brain rhythms remains poorly understood.

Recent advances in computational neuroscience have enabled researchers to simulate neural networks and investigate the behavior of neural oscillations and brain rhythms in a controlled environment. In this study, we employed a computational neuroscience approach to investigate the interplay between neural oscillations and brain rhythms in a simulated neural network. Our goal was to identify key features of neural oscillations that influence brain rhythms and to examine the implications of this interplay for understanding brain function.

## Methodology

We developed a theoretical model of a neural network, consisting of 10,000 neurons and 50,000 synapses, to simulate the behavior of neural oscillations and brain rhythms. The model was based on a modified version of the leaky integrate-and-fire (LIF) neuron model, which incorporates the effects of synaptic plasticity and short-term plasticity (Destexhe & Paré, 1999). We also employed a statistical approach, using a combination of power spectral density (PSD) analysis and Granger causality (GC) analysis to examine the relationships between different frequency bands of neural oscillations and brain rhythms.

The experimental setup consisted of a simulated neural network, which was exposed to a series of sensory stimuli, including visual, auditory, and tactile inputs. The network was then analyzed using the LIF model and statistical analysis tools, including PSD and GC analysis.

## Results

Our results show that the interaction between different frequency bands of neural oscillations generates complex brain rhythms, which in turn modulate neural activity. Specifically, we found that the alpha frequency band (8-12 Hz) was strongly correlated with the beta frequency band (13-30 Hz), while the gamma frequency band (30-100 Hz) was strongly correlated with the alpha frequency band. We also found that the brain rhythms generated by the interaction between these frequency bands were highly synchronized across different brain regions.

We used numerical simulations to examine the effects of different parameters on the interplay between neural oscillations and brain rhythms. Our results show that the strength of the interaction between frequency bands influences the complexity of brain rhythms and the modulation of neural activity. Specifically, we found that increasing the strength of the interaction between frequency bands resulted in more complex brain rhythms and greater modulation of neural activity.
