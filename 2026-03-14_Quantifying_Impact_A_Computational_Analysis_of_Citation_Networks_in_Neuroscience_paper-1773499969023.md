## Abstract

Citation analysis has become an essential tool for evaluating research impact in neuroscience. However, the rapidly growing volume of published research and the increasing interconnectedness of citation networks necessitate the development of more sophisticated methods for quantifying impact. Here, we present a computational analysis of citation networks in neuroscience, integrating graph theory, network analysis, and information theory. We introduce a novel impact metric, the "Citation Entropy Index" (CEI), which captures the diversity and centrality of an author's citation network. Our results demonstrate that CEI is a more robust predictor of research impact than traditional citation metrics, such as h-index and citation counts. The CEI framework also reveals the complex structure of citation networks in neuroscience, highlighting the importance of "bridge" authors who connect disparate communities. Our findings have implications for research evaluation, funding decisions, and the development of more effective knowledge diffusion strategies.

## Introduction

Citation analysis has long been a cornerstone of research evaluation in neuroscience, providing a quantitative measure of research impact. However, the rapidly growing volume of published research and the increasing interconnectedness of citation networks have rendered traditional citation metrics, such as h-index and citation counts, inadequate for capturing the complexity of research impact (Hirsch, 2005; Egghe, 2006). Recent advances in network analysis and information theory offer new opportunities for developing more sophisticated impact metrics (Newman, 2010; Cover & Thomas, 2012). Here, we present a computational analysis of citation networks in neuroscience, integrating graph theory, network analysis, and information theory. Our primary contribution is the introduction of a novel impact metric, the "Citation Entropy Index" (CEI), which captures the diversity and centrality of an author's citation network.

Our approach is motivated by the following key features of citation networks in neuroscience:

1. **Complexity**: Citation networks exhibit a high degree of complexity, with authors publishing across multiple disciplines and citation patterns reflecting the interconnectedness of these disciplines (Wuchty et al., 2007).
2. **Interconnectedness**: Citation networks in neuroscience are characterized by strong interconnections between authors, journals, and research topics, reflecting the collaborative and interdisciplinary nature of neuroscience research (Bornmann & Daniel, 2005).
3. **Heterogeneity**: Citation networks in neuroscience exhibit significant heterogeneity, with authors publishing across a wide range of topics, from molecular biology to behavioral neuroscience (Katz, 1953).

## Methodology

Our research approach integrates graph theory, network analysis, and information theory to develop a comprehensive understanding of citation networks in neuroscience. We employed the following methods:

1. **Data collection**: We collected citation data from the Web of Science database, covering the period from 2000 to 2020.
2. **Network construction**: We constructed citation networks using the collected data, representing authors as nodes and citations as edges.
3. **Network analysis**: We employed network analysis techniques, including degree centrality, betweenness centrality, and clustering coefficient, to characterize the structure of citation networks (Newman, 2010).
4. **Information theory**: We applied information theory concepts, including entropy and mutual information, to develop the CEI metric (Cover & Thomas, 2012).
